Electoral Reform Sponsors Belong to Two of Brazil's Most-Fined Political Parties

PSDB and MDB rank among the ten political parties that have accumulated the highest electoral fines over the past 15 years

Photo: Luiz Roberto/TSE

By Marcela Villar | 28 June 2026

Months before Brazil's presidential election, scheduled for October 2026, Brazil's Chamber of Deputies approved a bill without notice on the legislative agenda, which is considered a mini electoral reform.

One of the proposed changes would cap fines imposed by Brazil's Electoral Court at R$30,000 (about US$5,500)—a sharp reduction from the current rule established by the Political Parties Law. Today, when a party's financial statements are rejected, it must repay the amount deemed irregular in addition to a 20% fine.

The bill was introduced by federal representatives Pedro Lucas Fernandes (União Brasil), Adolfo Viana (PSDB) and Isnaldo Bulhões Jr (MDB).

Coincidentially, PSDB and MDB rank among the ten political parties that have accumulated the highest electoral fines over the past 15 years, according to an analysis of the most recent public data from Brazil's Superior Electoral Court (TSE) on annual party finance judgments.

The analysis covers 2010—when Brazil introduced its electronic finance reporting system—through 2024, the latest year available. Parties must submit their 2025 financial statements to the Electoral Court by June 30.

MDB, which is government-aligned, has accumulated R$16.2 million (about US$3.2 million) in fines, while PSDB, a center-right party, has accumulated R$5 million (about US$1 million).

The Workers' Party (PT), from President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's, tops the ranking with R$21.1 million (about US$4.2 million) in accumulated fines—roughly twice the historical average. Altogether, Brazil's 39 registered political parties have accumulated nearly R$157 million (about US$31 million) in electoral fines.

The bill would also introduce a statute of limitations for the Electoral Court to judge parties' financial statements—a deadline that currently does not exist. Under the proposal, a case would automatically prescribe if the court does not issue a ruling within one year.

According to TSE data, only 25% of annual financial reports have been judged within one year. On average, the court takes 3.7 years to reach a decision, while roughly one-third of cases have remained pending for more than 10 years.

Of the 731 annual financial statements analyzed, 61% remain pending.

The Brazilian Communist Party (PCB) had its financial statements rejected nine times between 2010 and 2024, the highest number among all political parties. It is followed by MOBILIZA (formerly PMN), PRTB, PCO, DC, PHS (now Podemos), PDT, Cidadania (formerly PPS), PTB, and DEM (now União Brasil).

Some parties failed to submit financial statements altogether. This occurred 10 times during the period analyzed. DEM (now União Brasil) and PCO each failed to file reports three times. PCB failed to submit reports twice, while PRP (later Patriota) and UP each failed to file once.

The bill would also require the automatic approval with reservations of financial statements when the identified irregularities do not exceed 10% of a party's annual revenue.

In addition, it would prohibit the seizure of resources from the Party Fund and the Special Campaign Finance Fund (FEFC) for any purpose. The proposal would also allow candidates to send automated campaign messages to previously registered phone numbers.

The bill was approved by the Chamber of Deputies on May 19 and is currently under consideration in the Senate (Bill 4822/2025). To become law, it must be approved by the Senate and then signed by the President.
